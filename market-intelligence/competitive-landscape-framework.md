# Competitive Landscape Framework — Knowing What You're Up Against

**Maintained by:** Chandra Buduri · [linkedin.com/in/chandrabuduri](https://linkedin.com/in/chandrabuduri)

> You can't win a talent competition you don't understand. This framework helps recruiting teams build a real-time picture of who they're competing against, what those companies are offering, and how to position effectively against them.

---

## The Four Dimensions of Competitive Landscape

Every talent market has four dimensions worth mapping. Most recruiters only look at one (comp). The best recruiting teams look at all four.

```
1. WHO  — Which companies are competing for the same candidates
2. WHAT — What they're offering (comp, equity, role, mission)
3. HOW  — How fast and how well they run their process
4. WHY  — What's drawing candidates to them (and away from you)
```

---

## Dimension 1: WHO — Competitor Identification

### Direct Competitors
Companies hiring for the exact same role, same level, same skills.

```markdown
## Direct Competitor Map: [Role] — [Date]

| Company | Stage | Funding | Headcount | # Similar open roles |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| [Co A] | Series C | $80M | 120 | 3 |
| [Co B] | Series B | $40M | 60 | 1 |
| [Co C] | Public | $2B ARR | 2,000 | 8 |

**Notes:**
- [Co A] just closed a round — expect aggressive hiring for next 6 months
- [Co C] is known for slow process (8–10 week average) — use this against them
```

### Indirect Competitors
Companies hiring for adjacent roles that pull from the same talent pool.

```markdown
## Indirect Competitor Map

| Company | Role they're hiring | Overlap with our target | Risk level |
|---|---|---|---|
| [Co D] | ML Platform Engineer | High — same skills, higher TC | High |
| [Co E] | AI Research Scientist | Medium — research vs. applied | Medium |
| [Co F] | Senior Backend Engineer | Low — different domain | Low |
```

### Passive Competitors
Companies not actively hiring but where your target candidates are happily employed.
These are your hardest conversations — you need a compelling reason for someone to leave stability.

```markdown
## Passive Competitor Map

| Company | Why candidates stay | What might make them leave |
|---|---|---|
| [Co G] | Strong brand, high TC, job security | Boredom, limited upside, slow career growth |
| [Co H] | Mission alignment, great team | Poor management, layoff risk, slow promotion |
| [Co I] | Equity event coming (pre-IPO) | Post-liquidity restlessness, culture drift |
```

## Dimension 3: HOW — Process Intelligence

Speed and quality of process is a significant differentiator that most companies ignore.

### Process Benchmarking

Gather this through:
- Glassdoor interview reviews
- Blind / Teamblind threads
- Candidate feedback in your own debriefs ("how did your other processes go?")
- Recruiter network conversations

```markdown
## Process Intelligence: Key Competitors

| Company | Avg process length | # Interview stages | Common complaints | Common praise |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| [Co A] | 3–4 weeks | 5 | Too many rounds, slow decisions | Strong technical bar |
| [Co B] | 6–8 weeks | 7 | Ghosting after onsites | Good candidate comms |
| [Co C] | 2–3 weeks | 4 | Lowball offers | Fast, respectful |

**Our process:** [X] weeks, [N] stages
**Our competitive position on process:** [Faster / Slower / Similar]
**Opportunity:** If [Co A] takes 6 weeks, we can win on speed alone if we close in 3.
```

### Process Speed as a Weapon

If your process is faster and better, say so explicitly in recruiter screens:

```
"One thing candidates tell us they appreciate is how we run our process.
We do [N] stages, we debrief within 24 hours, and from first call to offer
typically takes [X] weeks. We move fast because we respect your time."
```

## Dimension 4: WHY — Candidate Motivation Intelligence

Understanding why candidates choose competitors (or stay put) tells you how to position.

### Exit Interview Intelligence

Ask every declined candidate (respectfully, after the decision):

```
"I completely understand — and I appreciate you telling us directly.
Would you be willing to share what drove the decision?
Even rough feedback helps us improve."
```

Track patterns over 6–12 months:

```markdown
## Decline Reasons Tracker

| Reason | Count | % of declines | Action taken |
|---|---|---|---|
| Accepted competing offer (higher TC) | 8 | 40% | Comp band review |
| Role scope not compelling | 4 | 20% | JD and intake refresh |
| Company stage / risk concern | 3 | 15% | Equity story improvement |
| Process too slow | 2 | 10% | SLA enforcement |
| Relocated / not open to move | 2 | 10% | Remote policy review |
| Manager concern | 1 | 5% | Coaching with HM |
```

### Win/Loss Analysis (Quarterly)

Every quarter, review:
- Offers extended vs. accepted (acceptance rate)
- Where declined candidates went (competitor intelligence)
- What winning candidates said yes to (your real differentiators)

## Positioning Against Specific Competitor Types

### vs. Big Tech (Google, Meta, Apple, Microsoft, Amazon)

**Their strengths:** Brand, total comp, job security, resources
**Their weaknesses:** Slow process, bureaucracy, limited equity upside, less ownership

**Your positioning:**
```
"At [Company], you'd own this entire domain — not be one of 40 people working on a piece of it.
The equity upside here is real in a way it isn't at a public company where the stock price
has limited room to move. And you'll have a direct line to the founders every week."
```

### vs. Well-Funded Series B/C Startups

**Their strengths:** More resources, more brand recognition than you, competitive comp
**Their weaknesses:** Often more process, more politics, equity already diluted

**Your positioning:**
```
"We're earlier, which means the equity is more meaningful and the problems are less defined —
which is either exciting or not, depending on what you're looking for.
If you want to build something from scratch, earlier is better."
```

### vs. Public Companies (RSUs vs. Options)

**Their strengths:** Liquid equity, predictable total comp, lower risk
**Their weaknesses:** Options at their stage are worth far less as a % of company

**Your positioning:**
```
"RSUs are certain — but they're also priced in. You're getting a small slice of a very
large, fully-valued company. Our options are uncertain — but if we hit our targets,
you're looking at [X]x the value. Let me show you the math."
```

**Equity comparison script:**
```
"Let's compare the two offers side by side.
Their RSU grant is worth $[X] today — liquid, certain.
Our option grant: [N] options at $[Y] strike, current 409A is $[Z].
At our last round valuation, the grant is worth $[A] on paper.
If we exit at [3x / 5x / 10x] current valuation — which our investors underwrote —
your options are worth $[B / C / D].
The question is: do you believe in the outcome?"
```

## Competitive Intelligence Maintenance

A market map goes stale fast. Keep it current:

| Trigger | Action |
|---|---|
| Competitor raises a new round | Update comp estimates, expect more competition |
| Competitor announces layoffs | Update talent movement map, proactive outreach |
| New competitor enters the market | Add to landscape, assess threat level |
| Your offer acceptance rate drops | Run win/loss analysis immediately |
| Response rates to outreach drop | Market temperature has shifted — reassess |
| A strong candidate declines unexpectedly | Debrief deeply, update landscape |

**Recommended cadence:**
- Weekly: scan for competitor job postings, funding news, layoff announcements
- Monthly: update comp intelligence, review decline reasons
- Quarterly: full win/loss analysis and landscape refresh
