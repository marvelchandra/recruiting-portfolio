# Employer Branding Playbook — Building a Brand That Attracts Before You Recruit

**Maintained by:** Chandra Buduri · [linkedin.com/in/chandrabuduri](https://linkedin.com/in/chandrabuduri)

> At an early-stage company, you don't have brand recognition. A candidate who's never heard of you will make a judgment call in 30 seconds — based on your job description, your careers page, your recruiter's message, or what they find when they Google you. Employer branding at this stage is not a marketing campaign. It's about making sure every single touchpoint tells a coherent, honest, compelling story about why this company is worth the bet.

---

## Part 1: Brand Foundation — Define Before You Build

Everything else in this playbook depends on getting these answers right first. Do this exercise with the founders before writing a single JD or careers page word.

### Brand Foundation Workshop (60 min with Founders)

Run this as a conversation, not a form. Take notes. Look for the honest answers — not the polished ones.

```markdown
## Brand Foundation Questions

**On mission:**
What problem are you solving, and why does it matter?
(Push for specificity — "improving healthcare" is not an answer)

What would be lost if your company didn't exist?

Who are you building for, and why do they need you?

**On the work:**
What's technically interesting or hard about what you're building?
What would a strong engineer find genuinely exciting here?
What's still unsolved that your new hires will help figure out?

**On the team:**
Describe the 2–3 people on your team who represent your culture best.
What do they have in common?
What kind of person has failed here, and why?

**On the stage:**
What's great about being here right now?
What's genuinely hard about being here right now?
What does the next 18 months look like if everything goes right?

**On differentiation:**
Why would a strong candidate choose you over a well-funded competitor?
What can you offer that they can't?
(Be honest — if the answer is "nothing," that's important to know)
```

### The Brand Pillars (Output of the Workshop)

Distill the conversation into 4–5 brand pillars. These become the foundation of every external touchpoint.

```markdown
## [Company] Employer Brand Pillars

**Pillar 1: Mission**
[One sentence. Specific. Why this matters, for whom, right now.]
Example: "We're building the infrastructure that lets any lab run protein structure
prediction at the cost of a coffee — not a server farm."

**Pillar 2: Technical Challenge**
[What's genuinely hard and interesting about the work.]
Example: "The core problem is unsolved — we're not implementing known algorithms,
we're figuring out whether the approach even works."

**Pillar 3: Ownership & Autonomy**
[What does working here actually feel like vs. a larger company.]
Example: "You own your domain end-to-end. No PM telling you what to build.
No 6-month roadmap review to change a button color."

**Pillar 4: Team Quality**
[Why the people here are worth working with.]
Example: "Every engineer here has built something in production that people actually use.
We don't hire people who can only talk about what they'd do."

**Pillar 5: Equity Upside**
[Honest version of the financial case.]
Example: "We're Series A. The equity is real. If we execute, the outcome is meaningful
in a way it isn't at a company that's already priced in."
```

## Part 2: Job Descriptions

The JD is your highest-volume brand touchpoint. Every candidate evaluates it in under 60 seconds. Most startup JDs read like they were written by a committee of people who've never had to hire anyone.

### The JD Framework

```markdown
## [Role Title] — [Company Name]

**About [Company]** (3–4 sentences max)
What you do. Who you do it for. Why now. What stage you're at.
Be specific. Avoid: "We're changing the future of X." 
Write: "We build [specific thing] for [specific customer] — 
[N] of the top [Y] [companies/labs/hospitals] are already using it."

**The opportunity** (2–3 sentences)
Why is this role interesting RIGHT NOW at THIS company?
What will this person own that they couldn't own elsewhere?
What's the problem they'll be the primary person solving?

**What you'll do** (5–7 bullets)
Write outcomes, not tasks.
Bad: "Participate in architecture discussions"
Good: "Own the design and implementation of our real-time inference serving layer"
Bad: "Work closely with cross-functional teams"
Good: "Partner directly with ML researchers to take models from experiment to production"

**What we're looking for** (3–5 bullets — must-haves ONLY)
Every bullet should be a genuine dealbreaker.
If you'd still interview someone without it, it's not a must-have.
Separate must-haves from nice-to-haves explicitly.

**Nice to have** (2–3 bullets)
Real preferences. Not hidden requirements.

**Compensation**
Salary: $[X] – $[Y]
Equity: [X,000 – Y,000] options (Series [X] company, current 409A: $[Z])
[Benefits summary — keep it short]
[Remote / hybrid / onsite policy]

**Our interview process**
[List the stages and approximate timeline]
Example: "Recruiter screen (30 min) → HM conversation (45 min) →
Technical (60 min) → Virtual onsite (3 hrs) → Offer. 
We aim to complete the process in 3 weeks."
```

### JD Quality Checklist

Before posting any JD, run it through this checklist:

**Content:**
- [ ] Company description is specific — no generic mission statements
- [ ] The "why this role" section explains what makes it interesting right now
- [ ] Responsibilities are outcomes, not task lists
- [ ] Must-haves are genuine dealbreakers only (5 or fewer)
- [ ] Comp range is included (increases applicant quality, reduces waste)
- [ ] Interview process is described (candidates value transparency)

**Language:**
- [ ] No "rockstar," "ninja," "guru," "wizard," "unicorn"
- [ ] No "fast-paced environment" (says nothing)
- [ ] No "wear many hats" without context
- [ ] No "passionate" as a requirement
- [ ] No gendered language (run through a bias checker)
- [ ] No unnecessary degree requirements ("BS/MS/PhD required" when it isn't)
- [ ] No experience inflation ("10+ years of Python" for a 5-year-old language)
- [ ] Written in second person ("You will...") not third ("The candidate will...")

**Bias check:**
Run every JD through one of:
- Textio (paid, most comprehensive)
- Gender Decoder (free, gendered language only)
- Claude / ChatGPT prompt: "Review this JD for biased, exclusionary, or unnecessarily credentialist language"

### JD Anti-Patterns (Real Examples)

| What you wrote | What candidates read | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| "Fast-paced environment" | Chaotic, no process | "We ship weekly and move quickly — processes exist but don't slow us down" |
| "Wear many hats" | Understaffed, unclear scope | "This role owns X, Y, and Z — you'll have support from [team] on [adjacent areas]" |
| "Self-starter who thrives in ambiguity" | No onboarding, sink or swim | "You'll have a 30/60/90 day plan from day 1, and direct access to founders for context" |
| "10+ years required" | We'll screen out great candidates | Remove it. Use level descriptors instead. |
| "Excellent communication skills" | Filler | Remove it entirely or be specific: "Ability to write clearly for both technical and non-technical audiences" |
| "Competitive salary" | We don't want to show the number | Show the number. Full stop. |

## Part 3: Careers Page

Many early-stage companies have no careers page, or one that was built in an afternoon and forgotten. A strong careers page does two things: it gives candidates the context to self-select in (or out), and it reduces the recruiter's job because candidates arrive pre-sold.

### Minimum Viable Careers Page Structure

```
1. Hero section
   - What the company does (1 sentence, specific)
   - Why now (1 sentence on the market moment)
   - CTA: View open roles

2. Mission section
   - 2–3 paragraphs: the problem, the approach, the vision
   - Written like a human, not a press release

3. Team section
   - Photos and names of 5–10 team members
   - 1–2 lines on each person — what they built before, what they own now
   - Real photos. Not stock. Not headshots taken in 2012.

4. Working here section
   - 3–5 specific statements about how the team operates
   - Include what's hard, not just what's great
   - Example: "We don't have all the answers. If you need certainty to do your best work, this probably isn't the right fit. If you get energized by hard, open-ended problems, you'll love it here."

5. Benefits section
   - Health, dental, vision
   - Equity (frame it, don't hide it)
   - Remote / hybrid policy
   - Equipment stipend
   - Any standout perks (but only list real ones)

6. Open roles
   - Organized by function
   - Each role links to full JD
   - Updated in real-time (stale roles kill credibility)

7. Application CTA
   - Simple. Name, email, resume, optional cover letter.
   - No 47-field ATS forms.
   - "Don't see a role that fits? Send us a note anyway." (captures passive interest)
```

### Careers Page Copy Guidelines

**Do:**
- Write like a smart human talking to another smart human
- Be honest about stage and what's hard
- Show real team members with real backgrounds
- Update the page when roles change (stale = unprofessional)
- Include comp ranges in role listings (yes, on the careers page)

**Don't:**
- Use stock photos of diverse, smiling people in an open office
- Write "we're a family" (red flag to experienced candidates)
- List 30 open roles when you're a 20-person team (looks desperate)
- Use marketing copy ("revolutionizing," "disrupting," "transforming")
- Hide the company stage or investor information (candidates will find it anyway)

## Part 4: Recruiter Outreach as Brand

Every message a recruiter sends is a brand impression. A generic InMail does brand damage. A specific, researched message that references the candidate's actual work builds brand — even if they're not interested.

### The Outreach Quality Spectrum

**Brand-damaging (never send):**
```
Hi [First Name],

I came across your profile and was impressed by your background.
We're looking for talented engineers to join our exciting team.
Would you be open to learning more?

[Recruiter Name]
```

**Neutral (acceptable but forgettable):**
```
Hi [Name],

I'm recruiting for [Company], a [stage] startup working on [space].
We're hiring a [Role] and your background in [skill] caught my attention.

Would you be open to a 20-minute call?
```

**Brand-building (what to aim for):**
```
Hi [Name],

I read your paper on [specific topic] — the approach you took with
[specific detail] was genuinely interesting, particularly [why].

I'm recruiting for [Company]. We're a [stage] company working on
[specific problem]. The reason I'm reaching out is [specific connection
between their work and this role] — this isn't a generic fit.

The role owns [specific outcome]. Given your background in [specific thing],
I think you'd find it interesting — and I'd rather you tell me it's not
the right fit after a conversation than wonder.

Would you have 20 minutes this week?

[Name]
```

### Outreach Personalization at Scale

Personalization doesn't have to mean writing every message from scratch. Build a system:

**Tier 1 — Full personalization (top 20% of list)**
Research the candidate's specific work. Reference a paper, repo, talk, or project. Write entirely from scratch. Spend 10–15 minutes per message.

**Tier 2 — Semi-personalized (middle 50%)**
Use a template with personalization slots. Fill in: [specific company they work at], [specific skill or domain], [specific reason this role connects to their background]. Spend 3–5 minutes per message.

**Tier 3 — Template (bottom 30%)**
Clean, professional template. No fake personalization ("I came across your profile" when you clearly didn't). Be direct about why you're reaching out at scale: "We're building out our [function] team and your background in [domain] is exactly what we're looking for."

### Subject Lines That Work

For email outreach (not InMail):

```
[Their company] → [your company]: [specific role]
[Specific thing you noticed] — [Company] [Role]
A question about your work on [specific project/paper]
[Mutual connection] suggested I reach out
[Company] — [Role] that might interest you
```

Subject lines that don't work:
- "Exciting opportunity!" (spam)
- "Quick question" (vague, overused)
- "Are you open to new opportunities?" (lazy)
- "[Company] is hiring!" (not about them)

## Part 5: Glassdoor & LinkedIn Presence

Candidates check these before accepting interviews. If your Glassdoor has 2 reviews and one is a 1-star, that's a problem worth fixing before you spend money on sourcing.

### Glassdoor Management

**Building reviews proactively:**
- Ask new hires at their 30-day check-in: "Would you be willing to leave a Glassdoor review?"
- Ask departing employees who leave on good terms
- Make it easy: send them the direct link
- Never write fake reviews — it's detectable and devastating to brand

**Responding to negative reviews:**
Always respond. The response is read by future candidates as much as the review itself.

```markdown
## Response Framework for Negative Reviews

**Acknowledge:** Thank the reviewer for the feedback, even if you disagree with it.
"Thank you for taking the time to share this — we take all feedback seriously."

**Validate what's fair:** If there's truth in it, say so.
"You're right that our onboarding process was less structured during our rapid growth
phase last year."

**Describe what's changed:** If you've addressed it, say so specifically.
"We've since implemented a full 30/60/90 day onboarding plan for every new hire,
and added monthly all-hands for company-wide transparency."

**Don't be defensive:** Arguing with an anonymous reviewer in public always looks bad.
Never: "This review is inaccurate and does not reflect our culture."
Always: "We're sorry this was your experience — it doesn't reflect where we want to be."
```

### LinkedIn Company Page

**Keep current:**
- [ ] Company description updated (product, stage, investor)
- [ ] Employee count accurate (LinkedIn updates this from employee profiles)
- [ ] Logo and cover image professional
- [ ] Recent posts (at least 2–3/month minimum)
- [ ] Life tab populated with real photos and team content

**Content that performs well:**
- Engineering blog posts or technical deep-dives
- New hire announcements (with their permission)
- Product milestones and launches
- Founder/team perspective pieces
- Job openings (but not exclusively — ratio should be 3:1 content to jobs)

**Content to avoid:**
- Generic "we're hiring!" posts with no context
- Award announcements no one outside the company cares about
- Stock photos of "innovation"

## Part 6: Candidate Experience as Brand

Every stage of the interview process is a brand touchpoint. Candidates talk. A bad experience at stage 2 becomes a Glassdoor review and a warning to their network.

### Experience Standards by Stage

**Application:**
- Acknowledge within 2 business days (automated is fine)
- Rejection within 5 business days for clearly unqualified candidates
- No 47-field application forms — name, email, resume is enough

**Recruiter screen:**
- Confirm with agenda 24 hours before
- Start on time
- End with clear next steps and timeline
- Follow up within 24 hours with decision

**Technical / onsite:**
- Send prep materials 48 hours before
- Share interviewer names and LinkedIn profiles
- Send day-of logistics: link / location, parking, who to contact
- Debrief candidate within 48 hours — even if it's a no

**Offer stage:**
- Deliver offer verbally first, in writing same day
- Give 5+ business days to decide
- Check in midway through the decision window
- Never pressure-close

**Declined candidates:**
- Send a personal note, not an automated rejection
- Leave the door open: "If anything changes on either side, I hope we can reconnect"
- Stay connected on LinkedIn — declined candidates refer people and sometimes come back

### The Candidate NPS Question

At the end of every process — offer accepted or not — ask:

```
"On a scale of 1–10, how likely would you be to recommend our interview
process to a colleague, regardless of the outcome?"

Follow up: "What's the one thing we could do to make it better?"
```

Track this monthly. A candidate NPS below 7 is a brand problem, not just a process problem.

## Part 7: Employee Advocacy

Your best employer brand assets are the people already working there. Most companies completely underutilize this.

### Activating Employee Advocacy

**Make it easy:**
- Create a shared folder of company content employees can repost
- Draft LinkedIn posts they can use or adapt (some people want to share but don't know what to say)
- Tag employees in company posts so they can reshare with one click

**Employee spotlights:**
- Interview 1–2 employees per month about their work
- Post on LinkedIn and the careers page
- Ask: "What's the most interesting problem you've worked on here?"
- Real answers from real people outperform any marketing copy

**Referral as brand:**
A strong referral program is employer branding that pays for itself. When employees refer people, they're vouching for the company with their own reputation — the strongest possible endorsement.

**Technical content:**
Encourage engineers to blog, speak at meetups, publish to ArXiv, contribute to OSS.
Every technical contribution is employer brand. A researcher who publishes a paper with your company affiliation puts your name in front of every reader.

## Appendix: Employer Brand Audit Checklist

Run this audit when you start a new engagement or quarterly at an existing company:

**JDs:**
- [ ] All active JDs reviewed in last 30 days
- [ ] Comp ranges included
- [ ] Bias check completed
- [ ] No generic language or inflated requirements

**Careers page:**
- [ ] Updated in last 60 days
- [ ] Real team photos
- [ ] Open roles match actual open roles in ATS
- [ ] Company stage and investor info accurate

**Glassdoor:**
- [ ] All reviews responded to
- [ ] Rating above 4.0 (if below, investigate)
- [ ] Recent reviews (last 90 days) reviewed and addressed

**LinkedIn:**
- [ ] Company page updated
- [ ] Recent posts (last 30 days)
- [ ] Employee count accurate

**Outreach:**
- [ ] Outreach templates reviewed and updated
- [ ] Response rate tracked (target >25%)
- [ ] Personalization quality spot-checked

**Candidate experience:**
- [ ] SLAs being met (response time, debrief time)
- [ ] Candidate NPS tracked
- [ ] Recent feedback reviewed and actioned
